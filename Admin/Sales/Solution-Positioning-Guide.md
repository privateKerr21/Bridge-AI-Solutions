1. Current processes and workflows (step-by-step)

4. People involved (who touches it, handoffs)
5. Data sources and systems (where does info come from/go)
6. Error rates and rework (quality issues)
7. Strategic importance (why does this matter)

## Common Objections & Positioning Responses

### "This sounds expensive."

**Response:**
"I understand. Let me show you the math. You mentioned you're spending [X hours] per week on [task], and your time is worth $[Y] per hour. That's $[Z] in annual cost just in time alone, not counting the revenue opportunities you're missing. Our solution pays for itself in [timeframe] and then becomes pure profit."

**When to Use:**
- After quantifying their pain in discovery
- When you have clear ROI numbers
- Reframe as investment, not expense

---

### "We're not ready for AI yet."

**Response:**
"I totally get that AI can feel overwhelming. The way we approach it is different - you stay in full control. Think of it more like a very smart assistant that does the research and grunt work, and you make all the final decisions. You're not replacing your judgment, just getting back your time."

**When to Use:**
- Risk-averse clients (especially regulated industries)
- Emphasize human-in-the-loop design
- Position as augmentation, not replacement

---

### "We tried automation before and it didn't work."

**Response:**
"What happened?" [Listen, then] "That makes sense. A lot of automation tools are rigid and break when anything changes. What we build is different - we use AI agents that adapt and learn, not just rule-based workflows. Plus, we include ongoing optimization, so it gets better over time, not worse."

**When to Use:**
- After failed vendor experiences
- Differentiate AI from traditional automation
- Emphasize ongoing support and refinement

---

### "Can't you just do it cheaper?"

**Response:**
"I could lower the price, but I'd have to cut [specific value component]. For example, we could skip the ongoing optimization, but then you'd get 60% of the value instead of the full impact. Based on what you told me about [their pain point], I don't think that's in your best interest. The ROI is still [X]% even at this investment level."

**When to Use:**
- When they're focused on price instead of value
- Tie back to their specific pain points
- Show what they'd lose with cheaper option

**Alternative Response (if truly budget-constrained):**
"I understand budget is tight. What if we started with just [subset of solution] to prove the value, and then expanded once you see the ROI? We could do that for [lower investment]."

---

### "We need to think about it."

**Response:**
"Absolutely, that makes sense. Just so I can help with anything that might be holding you back - is this a timing issue, a budget issue, or is there something about the solution that doesn't feel like the right fit?"

**When to Use:**
- Uncover real objection hiding behind "think about it"
- Get specific on what's blocking them
- Give them permission to share concerns

---

### "We want to talk to other vendors first."

**Response:**
"That's smart - you should do your due diligence. As you talk to others, I'd recommend asking them about [specific differentiator: compliance design / human-in-the-loop / ongoing optimization]. That's where you'll see the biggest differences. Can I check back in with you in [timeframe] after you've had those conversations?"

**When to Use:**
- Competitive situations
- Plant seeds about your differentiators
- Stay in the conversation
