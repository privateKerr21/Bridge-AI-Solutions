## **ONE: Speak to their pain.**

No one cares what you know.  
Unless it can help them.

\#1 mistake is people make their content about them.

Look at me. Look at this. Look how cool I am.

That doesn’t work.

Ex:

Don’t start by saying “Here’s what I did...”, say “Here’s how this can help you...”

Then tell your story.

Those little tweaks change everything.

That being said, the easiest way to get customer \- is get them results way in advance of them every paying you.

Yes. I’m saying that. Give away your BEST stuff.

Information should be free.  
Implementation is paid.

So give all your information away.

Step 1: Talk to their pain.

The more you can explain their pain, BETTER than they can, then you become the expert.

That means starting by asking “Have you ever been on a diet but had to travel for work, and lost ALL your gains?”

That’s a pain.

The key is to make it non-obvious and observable.

Here’s 5 others:

*Dentist:*

*You laugh with your lips pressed tight... because one photo tag and your confidence vanishes for a week.*

*Fitness:*

*Your jeans button leaves a red mark so deep it follows you into the shower.*

*Marketing:*

*You spend hours on a post that your mom “likes”… and no one else even sees.*

*Your DMs look full... but it’s just ghosts who stopped replying after “what’s the price?”*

*Hiring:*

*You hand off a task and somehow it boomerangs back needing your help TWICE\!*

Use that in your content when you teach or share perspectives so it enrolls them into what you have to say.

Without a STRONG hook, no one watches.

It’s the Subject Line to your content. Nail it and they’ll stick around.

## **TWO: Formats are where social is won.**

No one will tell you this, but the best way to grow your socials is to find a format you like and your audience enjoys.

Some of my favourites are Career Ladder, Ice Bath Confessionals and Fake Facetimes.

The goal is to find a structure that your audience loves.

My friend Alan the fitness trainer, just shows ways to exercise a SPECIFIC muscle group and says it in a way that was non-obvious.

Here’s some other examples to get the juices flowing.

**Before & After Reveal:** Show the “before” scene (problem/state) then cut to the “after” (solution or transformation). Works for services, visual changes, or case studies.

**Listicle / Quick Tips:** Share 3–5 fast lessons or insights with numbered overlays while demonstrating. Easy saves and high educational value.

**Day-in-the-Life / Behind-the-Scenes:** Document your daily workflow, client process, or creative setup to build connection and authenticity.

**Challenge or Trend Participation**: Use a trending sound or format and adapt it to your niche. Increases reach and keeps your content current.

**Story-Time or Anecdote:** Speak directly to camera sharing a personal or client story that reveals a lesson or turning point.

**How-To / Mini Tutorial:** Teach one practical skill or workflow in under 30 seconds. Ideal for showing expertise and creating save-worthy content.

**Text-Over Video (No Talking):** Add captions or bold text over compelling visuals for viewers watching without sound.

**Jump-Cut Transformation / Mash-Up:** Use quick transitions or scene cuts to show progress, results, or multiple perspectives with visual impact.

**Skits / Relatable Drama:** Script a short, funny, or emotionally charged scenario that mirrors your audience’s real frustrations or desires.

**Product or Service Reveal:** Visually showcase your offer in action—product unboxing, customer reaction, or behind-the-scenes creation.

## **Using the A — B Method stretches the gap.**

Building urgency and conditioning for the sale is critical early in the conversation. 

We can utilize the A — B Method to consistently and effectively move the client from prospect \-\> interested \-\> committed.

The A—B Method is a sales tactic that requires the seller to collect three simple pieces of information in order to quickly uncover pain points that we can later use to close the deal.

1. Where is the business now? (Point A, Current State)  
     
2. Where would you like to be in 12 months? *Or…* Where SHOULD you be? (Point B, Ideal State)  
     
3. What is the primary roadblock between Point A and Point B? (Biggest challenge)   
     
4. What are 2-3 things missing to get to \[Desire result\]?  
     
5. What do you need most so you’re able to get to {outcome}?

## **The Buying Zone**

The Buying Zone is the mental and emotional zone where a prospect is most likely to make a decision and invest. It’s the space between their current reality (Version 1\) and their desired reality (Version 2).

The point where internal tension is highest and action feels necessary.

There are 2 rules to remember:

1. They can’t be so close to Version 2 they believe they can do it alone.  
2. They can’t be so far from Version 2 that they’ve lost hope.

When both are true… belief in possibility and belief in need… that’s the Buying Zone.

**How It Works**

Your job as a salesperson is to diagnose where the person sits on that spectrum and move them into the Zone through conversation:

If they’re too far left (hopeless), you reframe their outlook…

Showing them that success is easier than they think and reignite belief.  
Here’s how:

1. **Shift blame from self to strategy**

   “It’s not that you can’t do it… it’s that your plan was never built to work long term.”

2. **Reduce the mountain to one clear step**

   “Let’s forget the next 12 months. if you only focused on fixing this one thing first, would that make life easier?”

3. **Anchor hope with proof**

   “Had someone just like you last month. same story, we fixed one piece and everything started moving again.”

4. **Reframe failure as data**

“Nothing you’ve tried was a waste… it showed us exactly what   
doesn’t work, so we can skip the trial-and-error.”

5. **Transfer certainty through partnership**

“You don’t need to have it all figured out. Leave that to me. You just   
need to be willing to execute.”

If they’re too far right (ego, “I can do it myself”), you anchor them back to pain and cost of inaction.

Here’s how: 

1. **Validate their competence first**

“Makes sense, you’ve got the drive and experience. Most people don’t even get this far.”  
(Disarms ego by giving them status before redirecting.)

2. **Expose the gap**

   “Out of curiosity, if it’s working, what’s stopping you from already hitting \[specific result\]?” (Forces them to confront the delta between effort and outcome… without arguing.)

3. **Reframe independence as limitation**

“You’re doing great, and that can get a lot of people stuck. Cause being the one who does everything keeps you from building what actually scales.”  
(Turns their pride point into a performance bottleneck.)

4. **Anchor pain to opportunity cost**

   “Every month this stays where it is, that’s another $X left on the table. Even if you’re winning, you could still be winning more.”

(Re-connects them to the cost of inaction — the silent pain.)

5. **Offer collaboration, not correction**

“My role isn’t to take over; it’s to multiply what’s already working.”  
(Protects their ego while reframing your offer as an upgrade, not a   
rescue.)

Once they enter the middle… the Buying Zone… they experience what “internal pressure”:

If someone is outside the Buying Zone, don’t make an offer.

The closer you can guide a prospect in the Buying Zone, the more effortless the close feels… they convince themselves, not you.

### **Core Principal \#4: Master The 9 Buyer Types**

Every prospect you meet isn’t just a “lead” 

Each type has its own resistance pattern, decision biases, and emotional trigger that shapes how they buy… or don’t.

When you understand which buyer type you’re talking to, you stop guessing what to say and start speaking directly to how they think.

Here’s the 9 Buyer Types and how to adjust your tone, pacing, and framing so every message lands exactly where it needs to: their belief system.

**1\. The Sprinter**

Psychology: Values time, hates friction, makes quick calls if it feels right.

Signals: “Just tell me what I need to know,” “How fast can we get started?”

“Alright, what’s  the fastest way we can get this rolling?”

“Send me the link — I’ll sign today if it makes sense.”

“Just give me the bullet points. I don’t need all the fluff.”

How to Close: Keep it short. Show them the fastest path to ROI. Remove every roadblock.

Advanced Reframe: Pace them just enough to prevent buyer’s remorse while feeding their bias for action.

Reframe: “Love that you’re an action taker, let’s make sure this is the right action for you.

Move them from impulse speed → sustained momentum.

**2\. The Skeptic**

Psychology: Doubts everything. Pride in not being fooled.

Signals: Pushes back hard, questions motives, plays devil’s advocate.

“Why should I believe you? Everyone says they’re the best.”

“This sounds too good to be true. What aren’t you telling me?”

“I’ve heard pitches like this before — prove you’re different.”

How to Close: Acknowledge skepticism openly. Use radical transparency. Turn their doubt into curiosity.

Advanced Reframe: Leverage their skepticism as a strength to be proud of.

Reframe: “Good — you should be skeptical. Skepticism is 100% acceptable until it turns into self sabotage.”

Move them from resistant doubter → proud, discerning decision-maker.

**3\. The Analyzer**

Psychology: Needs certainty, facts, and a clear path. Buys with the head first.

Signals: Asks about data, guarantees, track record, process.

“Can you walk me through the ROI numbers again?”

“What’s the exact step-by-step process once we start?”

“How does your solution compare against \[competitor\]?”

How to Close: Stack undeniable proof, frameworks, and numbers. Use case studies and risk reversal.

**Advanced Reframe: Instead of drowning them in logic, show how emotion is the logical choice.**

**Reframe: “The most rational decision is to stop overthinking and start compounding results.”**

**4\. The Dreamer**

Psychology: Buys on desire, identity, and vision. They want to feel something.

Signals: Talks about “how it feels,” “vibe,” or “energy.” Responds to stories.

How to Close: Paint vivid “after” scenarios. Anchor the decision to their identity and transformation.

“I just want to feel excited about this again.”

“Honestly, I’ve been stuck for years — I need something that lights me up.”

“This feels like the kind of thing I’ve been waiting for.”

**Advanced Reframe: Anchor their feelings to identity permanence.**

**Reframe: “This isn’t about feeling good today — it’s about becoming the kind of person who doesn’t settle.”**

**5\. The Fearful Buyer**

Psychology: Hates loss more than they love gain.

Signals: Stuck on “what if this doesn’t work?” or past failures.

“What happens if this doesn’t work for me?”

“I’ve tried programs before and they burned me.”

“What’s the worst-case scenario here?”

How to Close: Show cost of inaction. Reframe risk so doing nothing becomes the bigger danger.

**Fear of the past → Fear of the future**  
**Move them from risk-averse → risk-aware of doing nothing.**

**6\. The Seeker**

Psychology: Seeks validation from others (team, partner, peers).

Signals: “I need to check with…” “What do others say?”

“I need to run this past my business partner first.”

“What do other clients say about you?”

“Do you have testimonials from companies in my industry?”

How to Close: Arm them with social proof, testimonials, and authority signals so they can sell it internally.

Advanced Reframe: Frame them as the influencer of their circle rather than the follower.

**Reframe: “Your people are going to look to you. Do you want to show them hesitation, or leadership?”**

**7\. The Status Buyer**

Psychology: Wants to win, look powerful, or elevate reputation. They buy to signal.

Signals: Mentions peers, competitors, or being “ahead of the curve.”

How to Close: Use exclusivity, scarcity, and positioning. Frame it as a status-elevating move.

“I don’t want to be the last one in my circle to figure this out.”

“My peers are already doing this — I can’t afford to lag behind.”

“My competition is ahead” 

**Advanced Reframe: Flip it so they see not acting as status loss.**

- **Reframe: “The leaders in your space don’t wait. They move first.”**

**8\. The Connector**

Psychology: Trust \> everything. They buy the person before the product.

Signals: Asks about you, values rapport, slow to decide without strong connection.

“I really like your vibe — I can tell you actually care.”

“I don’t make decisions unless I fully trust who I’m working with.”

“Can we talk a bit more about your story? I want to know who I’m dealing with.”

How to Close: Build deep trust. Share personal stories. Demonstrate loyalty and long-term commitment.

**Reframe You’re not buying me — you’re buying certainty in moments you can’t trust yourself. That’s what changes everything.**

**9\. The Commander**

Psychology: Needs autonomy and assurance they’re making the decision, not being pushed.

Signals: Questions authority, wants options, skeptical of being “sold.”

“I’ll make the decision when I’m ready — don’t push me.”

“Do I get to customize this, or is it all rigid?”

“I don’t like being told what to do. How much freedom will I have?”

How to Close: Offer choices. Position yourself as a guide, not a pusher. Give them ownership of the decision.

**Reframe:** “My role isn’t to tell you what to do… it’s to give you the clearest path so you make the most powerful decision for yourself.”

# **CLOSING**

Your role as a closer consists of 6 core responsibilities:

1. Quickly and seamlessly take ownership of conversations passed to you by the opener.  
     
2. Ask direct questions to gain crystal clear understanding of:  
   1. Where they are in the business now  
   2. Where they’d like the business to be in the future (usually 12 months from now)  
   3. What’s stopping them from being there?  
        
3. Challenge limiting beliefs and their commitment to accomplishing their goals.  
     
4. Draw out and pre-handle objections before making the offer.  
     
5. Make the offer.  
     
6. Follow up relentlessly until confirmation of enrollment.  
     
* **Note:** Chats should be passed along only after the Opener has confirmed qualification, established a relationship, and found (at least) the tip of the iceberg of the client’s pain point. Now, we dig deeper.

## **Current State**

- What are you currently doing to {get to outcome}?  
- How satisfied are you with {your progress, outcomes, process}?  
- Where you at today on those dimensions?  
- How do you generate leads now?  
- How satisfied are you with {current situation}?

* **Note:** Make them feel seen and heard by mirroring their pain/goals back to them.

- I get that… but growth doesn’t have to mean more hours  
- Working on building better systems… I see   
- Need funding… ok   
- Working on scaling… 100%  
- Building authority… yup  
- Uh oh  
- Oh no

## **Desired State**

Determine where they want to be / what their goals are and suggest we can help.

- In 12 months, what kind of life would you like to create?  
- What’s the big goal with it?  
- What kind of lifestyle would that allow you?  
- How will you spend your time?

# **OBJECTION HANDLING**

### **Secret 1: Act like there’s a line of people waiting for you to change their life.**

Most salespeople panic when someone hesitates…

Like this was their only shot. 

But when you mentally picture a mile-long line of buyers who want what you have… your energy shifts.

You stop chasing and you start leading.

You don’t need to fake scarcity or lie about X spots left.

The world is full of people in pain, and you’re the one holding the medicine. 

This stance alone changes your words, and your certainty.

**Secret 2: You don’t have to outrun the bear, just the guy next to you.**

Most closers think they’re competing against every other offer. You’re not.

You’re competing against inaction.

When a prospect hesitates, they’re not comparing your offer to someone else’s, they’re comparing it to doing nothing.

So your job isn’t to convince them you’re the best. It’s to show them what it’s costing them to stay the same.

Help them feel that doing nothing is the real cost. Once they see that clearly, they’ll move.

**Secret 3: Never make them feel wrong for hesitating.**

If they feel judged, they’ll protect their ego instead of making a decision.

Your certainty invites their trust, but your empathy keeps them open.

When you combine the posture of abundance (Secret 1\) with the awareness of pain (Secret 2\) without making them feel small, that’s when objections dissolve.

**Secret 4: Objections don’t show resistance, they show interest.**

Someone giving you objections mean they’re STILL thinking about buying.

They’re trying to switch desire with fear.

When someone says, “I need to think about it,” what they really mean is, “I want to, but I’m scared to choose wrong.”

Your job isn’t to bulldoze that fear… it’s to name it, normalize it, and help them feel safe moving through it.  
Once they feel understood, fear loses its power.

**Secret 5: Don’t handle objections… reframe them.**

The pros don’t “answer” objections. They reframe them.

Because the moment you “answer,” you validate that the objection is the real issue.

Instead, you reframe it.

For example:

“It’s expensive” → “Totally. Most people who said that at first later realized it’s more expensive not to fix this.”

“I need time to think” → “Of course. Usually that means you’re thinking if this is the right move for you… what part feels unclear?”

Reframing keeps the power on your side. It doesn’t reject their concern \- it redefines it in a way that moves them forward.

## **The Buying Zone**

The Buying Zone is the mental and emotional zone where a prospect is most likely to make a decision and invest. It’s the space between their current reality (Version 1\) and their desired reality (Version 2).

The point where internal tension is highest and action feels necessary.

There are 2 rules to remember:

1. They can’t be so close to Version 2 they believe they can do it alone.  
2. They can’t be so far from Version 2 that they’ve lost hope.

When both are true… belief in possibility and belief in need… that’s the Buying Zone.

**How It Works**

Your job as a salesperson is to diagnose where the person sits on that spectrum and move them into the Zone through conversation:

If they’re too far left (hopeless), you reframe their outlook…

Showing them that success is easier than they think and reignite belief.  
Here’s how:

1. **Shift blame from self to strategy**

   “It’s not that you can’t do it… it’s that your plan was never built to work long term.”

2. **Reduce the mountain to one clear step**

   “Let’s forget the next 12 months. if you only focused on fixing this one thing first, would that make life easier?”

3. **Anchor hope with proof**

   “Had someone just like you last month. same story, we fixed one piece and everything started moving again.”

4. **Reframe failure as data**

“Nothing you’ve tried was a waste… it showed us exactly what   
doesn’t work, so we can skip the trial-and-error.”

5. **Transfer certainty through partnership**

“You don’t need to have it all figured out. Leave that to me. You just   
need to be willing to execute.”

If they’re too far right (ego, “I can do it myself”), you anchor them back to pain and cost of inaction.

Here’s how: 

1. **Validate their competence first**

“Makes sense, you’ve got the drive and experience. Most people don’t even get this far.”  
(Disarms ego by giving them status before redirecting.)

2. **Expose the gap**

   “Out of curiosity, if it’s working, what’s stopping you from already hitting \[specific result\]?” (Forces them to confront the delta between effort and outcome… without arguing.)

3. **Reframe independence as limitation**

“You’re doing great, and that can get a lot of people stuck. Cause being the one who does everything keeps you from building what actually scales.”  
(Turns their pride point into a performance bottleneck.)

4. **Anchor pain to opportunity cost**

   “Every month this stays where it is, that’s another $X left on the table. Even if you’re winning, you could still be winning more.”

(Re-connects them to the cost of inaction — the silent pain.)

5. **Offer collaboration, not correction**

“My role isn’t to take over; it’s to multiply what’s already working.”  
(Protects their ego while reframing your offer as an upgrade, not a   
rescue.)

Once they enter the middle… the Buying Zone… they experience what “internal pressure”:

If someone is outside the Buying Zone, don’t make an offer.

The closer you can guide a prospect in the Buying Zone, the more effortless the close feels… they convince themselves, not you.

### **Core Principal \#4: Master The 9 Buyer Types**

Every prospect you meet isn’t just a “lead” 

Each type has its own resistance pattern, decision biases, and emotional trigger that shapes how they buy… or don’t.

When you understand which buyer type you’re talking to, you stop guessing what to say and start speaking directly to how they think.

Here’s the 9 Buyer Types and how to adjust your tone, pacing, and framing so every message lands exactly where it needs to: their belief system.

**1\. The Sprinter**

Psychology: Values time, hates friction, makes quick calls if it feels right.

Signals: “Just tell me what I need to know,” “How fast can we get started?”

“Alright, what’s  the fastest way we can get this rolling?”

“Send me the link — I’ll sign today if it makes sense.”

“Just give me the bullet points. I don’t need all the fluff.”

How to Close: Keep it short. Show them the fastest path to ROI. Remove every roadblock.

Advanced Reframe: Pace them just enough to prevent buyer’s remorse while feeding their bias for action.

Reframe: “Love that you’re an action taker, let’s make sure this is the right action for you.

Move them from impulse speed → sustained momentum.

**2\. The Skeptic**

Psychology: Doubts everything. Pride in not being fooled.

Signals: Pushes back hard, questions motives, plays devil’s advocate.

“Why should I believe you? Everyone says they’re the best.”

“This sounds too good to be true. What aren’t you telling me?”

“I’ve heard pitches like this before — prove you’re different.”

How to Close: Acknowledge skepticism openly. Use radical transparency. Turn their doubt into curiosity.

Advanced Reframe: Leverage their skepticism as a strength to be proud of.

Reframe: “Good — you should be skeptical. Skepticism is 100% acceptable until it turns into self sabotage.”

Move them from resistant doubter → proud, discerning decision-maker.

**3\. The Analyzer**

Psychology: Needs certainty, facts, and a clear path. Buys with the head first.

Signals: Asks about data, guarantees, track record, process.

“Can you walk me through the ROI numbers again?”

“What’s the exact step-by-step process once we start?”

“How does your solution compare against \[competitor\]?”

How to Close: Stack undeniable proof, frameworks, and numbers. Use case studies and risk reversal.

**Advanced Reframe: Instead of drowning them in logic, show how emotion is the logical choice.**

**Reframe: “The most rational decision is to stop overthinking and start compounding results.”**

**4\. The Dreamer**

Psychology: Buys on desire, identity, and vision. They want to feel something.

Signals: Talks about “how it feels,” “vibe,” or “energy.” Responds to stories.

How to Close: Paint vivid “after” scenarios. Anchor the decision to their identity and transformation.

“I just want to feel excited about this again.”

“Honestly, I’ve been stuck for years — I need something that lights me up.”

“This feels like the kind of thing I’ve been waiting for.”

**Advanced Reframe: Anchor their feelings to identity permanence.**

**Reframe: “This isn’t about feeling good today — it’s about becoming the kind of person who doesn’t settle.”**

**5\. The Fearful Buyer**

Psychology: Hates loss more than they love gain.

Signals: Stuck on “what if this doesn’t work?” or past failures.

“What happens if this doesn’t work for me?”

“I’ve tried programs before and they burned me.”

“What’s the worst-case scenario here?”

How to Close: Show cost of inaction. Reframe risk so doing nothing becomes the bigger danger.

**Fear of the past → Fear of the future**  
**Move them from risk-averse → risk-aware of doing nothing.**

**6\. The Seeker**

Psychology: Seeks validation from others (team, partner, peers).

Signals: “I need to check with…” “What do others say?”

“I need to run this past my business partner first.”

“What do other clients say about you?”

“Do you have testimonials from companies in my industry?”

How to Close: Arm them with social proof, testimonials, and authority signals so they can sell it internally.

Advanced Reframe: Frame them as the influencer of their circle rather than the follower.

**Reframe: “Your people are going to look to you. Do you want to show them hesitation, or leadership?”**

**7\. The Status Buyer**

Psychology: Wants to win, look powerful, or elevate reputation. They buy to signal.

Signals: Mentions peers, competitors, or being “ahead of the curve.”

How to Close: Use exclusivity, scarcity, and positioning. Frame it as a status-elevating move.

“I don’t want to be the last one in my circle to figure this out.”

“My peers are already doing this — I can’t afford to lag behind.”

“My competition is ahead”

- **Reframe: “The leaders in your space don’t wait. They move first.”**

**8\. The Connector**

Psychology: Trust \> everything. They buy the person before the product.

Signals: Asks about you, values rapport, slow to decide without strong connection.

“Can we talk a bit more about your story? I want to know who I’m dealing with.”

**9\. The Commander**

# **OBJECTION HANDLING**

### **Secret 1: Act like there’s a line of people waiting for you to change their life.**

Most salespeople panic when someone hesitates…

Like this was their only shot. 

But when you mentally picture a mile-long line of buyers who want what you have… your energy shifts.

You stop chasing and you start leading.

You don’t need to fake scarcity or lie about X spots left.

The world is full of people in pain, and you’re the one holding the medicine. 

This stance alone changes your words, and your certainty.

**Secret 2: You don’t have to outrun the bear, just the guy next to you.**

Most closers think they’re competing against every other offer. You’re not.

You’re competing against inaction.

When a prospect hesitates, they’re not comparing your offer to someone else’s, they’re comparing it to doing nothing.

So your job isn’t to convince them you’re the best. It’s to show them what it’s costing them to stay the same.

Help them feel that doing nothing is the real cost. Once they see that clearly, they’ll move.

**Secret 3: Never make them feel wrong for hesitating.**

If they feel judged, they’ll protect their ego instead of making a decision.

Your certainty invites their trust, but your empathy keeps them open.

When you combine the posture of abundance (Secret 1\) with the awareness of pain (Secret 2\) without making them feel small, that’s when objections dissolve.

**Secret 4: Objections don’t show resistance, they show interest.**

Someone giving you objections mean they’re STILL thinking about buying.

They’re trying to switch desire with fear.

When someone says, “I need to think about it,” what they really mean is, “I want to, but I’m scared to choose wrong.”

Your job isn’t to bulldoze that fear… it’s to name it, normalize it, and help them feel safe moving through it.  
Once they feel understood, fear loses its power.

**Secret 5: Don’t handle objections… reframe them.**

The pros don’t “answer” objections. They reframe them.

Because the moment you “answer,” you validate that the objection is the real issue.

Instead, you reframe it.

For example:

“It’s expensive” → “Totally. Most people who said that at first later realized it’s more expensive not to fix this.”

“I need time to think” → “Of course. Usually that means you’re thinking if this is the right move for you… what part feels unclear?”

Reframing keeps the power on your side. It doesn’t reject their concern \- it redefines it in a way that moves them forward.

Now let’s look at the most common objections:

**I have to talk to my spouse about it.**

Acknowledge the importance of the partners input 

*"I completely understand.* 

*It’s important to ensure that both of you are on the same page."*

Isolate & Confirm they want to do it

*“Besides getting the green light from your spouse, do you feel we can get you to (result)?”*

Probe \- sometimes this objection is a polite way to delay or avoiding making a decision

*“What do you think their main concern will be?”*  
*“What happens if you go to them and they say no?”*

More often than not, the objection given is not the spouses, but THEIRS.

Challenge them

*“You’re a business owner*

*You make 100’s of business decisions a day*

*Why is this one different?*

*My concern is… Are you always going to need your spouses permission for every business decision?”*

**I’m too busy right now.**

Acknowledge their busyness 

*"No issue. It sounds like you’ve got a lot on your plate right now. And that’s the reason why people do this in the first place.”*

*Isolate the objection*

*“Besides finding the time to do this, do you feel this will help?”*

Position as a time saver, not a drain*.*

*“One of the main benefits of the product is to free up your time*

*Get you working on the business not in it. Clients typically free up X hours doing this… do you want that?”*

“**It’s too expensive**” **or** “**I can’t afford it right now**.”

Reframe coaching as an investment, never a cost or expense. Focus on the ROI.

*“I can totally understand where you're coming from.*

*Though may I ask, is money the only issue holding you back from working together?* 

*Or do you have any other concerns?*

*Said in another way, if money was not an issue, are you ready to get started now?”*

*If they say yes…*

*“Then if we were to break it up into something more digestible, would that help?”*

*If they say no…*

*“Then I can understand that you’re concerned about getting results…”*

*“From your perspective what’s more expensive? Is it more expensive for you to keep having these challenges? Or is it more expensive to invest in your growth so we can fix them together?*

\*Price is usually only an objection if you failed to demonstrate the value (rushed to offer)\* 

**It’s not the right time**

Probe deeper \- get specific. Ask questions to better understand what specifically makes the timing feel challenging.

*"Can you share what makes it feel like timing might not be right?*

*So for you how much of a priority is fixing {XYZ} challenges?*

*"I’m sorry to hear that your previous experience wasn’t helpful.”*

Dig deeper into their previous experience. Get specific. 

*“Can I ask what specifically didn’t work or what felt unhelpful in your previous experience?”*

Find the missing link and make it a core part of your offer

*“Ah so you didn’t have a step by step plan to follow.*

*If you had that in place now, do you feel you’d be able to get results?*

Leverage existing content

Reframe their solution-based thinking to pain-based thinking
