# LinkedIn Post — Your AI Project Failed. The AI Wasn't the Problem.

"The AI hallucinated."

"It wasn't smart enough."

"It kept getting edge cases wrong."

This is the comment section under every failed-AI-build post on Reddit. r/smallbusiness, r/agency, the operator threads on LinkedIn — same pattern. Real money spent, system shelved eight months later, team back on the old process.

The instinct is to blame the AI.

When you read past the headline, the AI almost never failed the way people remember. The model produced output. The output was wrong — but the reason lives upstream of the model, not inside it.

Three patterns show up over and over:

The data was messier than anyone admitted. Different spellings of the same client, files in three tools, "source of truth" depends on who you ask. The AI inherits all of that.

The process only existed in someone's head. The person who handles it gives the 80% version when asked. The other 20% — the exceptions, the "if it's that client we do this instead" — only surfaces when they're actually doing the work. The model never gets told that 20% exists.

Nobody defined what "working" looks like. "We want to automate proposals" is not a success metric. Without one, every imperfect output becomes evidence the project failed. The wins get forgotten because nobody tracked them.

The technology is mature enough. The bottleneck is the inputs.

Clean the data. Document the process. Define the outcome. Then build.

Skip that and you're not buying AI. You're buying a more expensive way to discover the problems you already had.

Where do most SMBs get tripped up — data, process, or expectations?
